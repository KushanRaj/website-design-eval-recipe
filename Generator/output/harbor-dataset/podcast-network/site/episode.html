"There are rooms that learn your breathing."

00:04
Mara Quan

It is twenty-two-oh-four on a Sunday in Brooklyn. We are in Studio B, the lights are at about thirty percent, and there is a magenta neon on the wall that I am pretending not to be vain about. August is here. We are talking to Nia Okwere tonight, who has driven up from Athens, Ohio, with — I want to say a thermos and a notebook.

00:34
August Vellor

Two notebooks. One for the room, one for the tape.

00:42
Nia Okwere

My father built radios. Mostly transistor sets in the 1970s, in Lagos. The first thing I ever learned about a room is that you can change one — drastically — by hanging a wet bath towel in the corner of it. It will sound like a different room. The towel is doing some math with the high end that no plugin has ever convinced me of. I have spent the last six years building one room very carefully. I want to talk about that, and I want to talk about why I think a microphone is a kind of confession booth.

04:18
Mara Quan

Let's start with the chapel. You bought a deconsecrated chapel outside Athens in — twenty-twenty?

04:25
Nia Okwere

September of twenty-twenty. It was nine thousand dollars and one congregation's worth of mildew. The pews were gone. The pipe organ was — let's say "removed" — by the time I got there. But the windows were intact, the apse was intact, and there was a small graveyard that, importantly, was not mine.

22:40
Nia Okwere

There are rooms that listen to you. There are rooms that learn your breathing. Most of them are accidents — corners, drapes, old carpet — but a few are intentional, and that is what I have been chasing in Athens. I do not believe a recording is a transcription of an event. I believe a recording is a transcription of an event and a room having an opinion about that event. The chapel has very strong opinions. It does not like cymbals. It loves a contralto. It treats a Wurlitzer the way a librarian treats a person eating soup.

47:00
August Vellor

And this is the moment Joel comes in with a tape from inside the chapel — recorded last Tuesday at — what time, Joel?

47:09
Joel Imani

Three fifty-two in the morning. I have a binaural rig in the apse and I let it run for an hour. What I'm about to play is six minutes from the middle of that. There are no instruments. There is a furnace, twice. There is a screech owl. There is, briefly, an HVAC fan kicking on. I want everyone to listen to what the building does to that fan.

59:12
Mara Quan

Nia, you wanted to play one piece for us in full tonight. Do you want to set it up?

59:24
Nia Okwere

It is for solo prepared piano. I recorded it in the chapel last winter at four in the morning. I want you to hear the room more than the piano. I think you will find that the piano allows it.

01:08:00
August Vellor

One letter from the mailbag before we go — this is from K. in Halifax, who writes: "I started listening because I have trouble sleeping. I keep listening because I find I am taking notes about it the next morning." That is — we want that. Keep writing to us. Goodnight from Brooklyn.
