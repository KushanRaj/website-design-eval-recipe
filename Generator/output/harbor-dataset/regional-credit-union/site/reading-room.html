The plain math of a 15-year mortgage

A worked example. Why a payment that looks larger can be the friendlier loan over a working life.

By Hua Liang, CPA · Member Services Editor

The first thing to admit, plainly, is that a 15-year mortgage has a larger monthly payment than a 30-year mortgage on the same house. There is no maneuver to make that arithmetic disappear. But once a member sits across from us at the table and the numbers are written out by hand, three things tend to emerge.

First, the interest paid over the life of the loan. Consider a $245,000 mortgage. At today's 30-year rate of 6.125%, the monthly principal-and-interest payment is roughly $1,489. Multiplied out over 360 months: $536,116 paid; $291,116 of that is interest. The same loan at the 15-year rate of 5.50% has a monthly payment of $2,001 — meaningfully larger — but the total paid over the life of the loan is $360,222, of which only $115,222 is interest. A difference of $175,894.

That is not a small number. That is a college education, a new roof, a meaningful retirement contribution.[1]

Second, equity accrual. In the first year of a 30-year loan, roughly 14% of each payment goes to principal. In the first year of a 15-year loan, that figure is 49%. From day one you are paying down the house, not renting the capital.

Third, the psychological matter. A 15-year mortgage finishes when your kindergartener is leaving for college. A 30-year mortgage doesn't.

We are not in the business of telling members which to choose; we are in the business of putting the math in front of them. Our calculator workshop on the next page does the calculations live. Our lenders, in branch, will do them on a legal pad if you prefer.

One caveat: this argument assumes you can comfortably afford the larger payment. If a 15-year payment crowds out an emergency fund, retirement contribution, or any cushion, the 30-year is the right loan. The friendliest mortgage is the one you can keep paying.

Feature · Member Services

When to call us before a debt collector does

A practical timeline from our member-services lead. Earlier than you think.

Many members tell us, after the fact, that they wish they had called the day they realized a payment was going to be missed — not the week after, not the month after, and certainly not after the second collection letter.

Our policy is plainly stated: if you call us before your account is 30 days past due, we have twelve distinct tools to help. After 60 days, eight. After 90, four. After a collection referral, two.

The reason is not punitive: the cooperative loses optionality alongside you. Calling early is, frankly, the single best thing you can do for your credit history and our shared balance sheet.

Call our member-services desk at the number on the back of your card. Saturday hours, 9 to 1, are answered by a real person in Linfield.

By Inez Park · Member Services Lead

Feature · Agriculture

A farm changes hands: succession finance, briefly

How the Klauser family used a five-year transition plan to keep three generations on the same orchard.

Of the 312 family farms in our service area, our ag-lending team estimates that 47 will face a generational handover in the next decade. About a third will succeed without a structured plan; the rest will benefit from one.

The Klauser family — orchardists in the Upper Basin since 1929 — opted for a five-year staged transition: Tomás (third generation) acquired the cold-storage building outright in year one, took on operational control in year two, and assumed the deed in year five. We carried equipment loans against each phase.

The arrangement let the second generation step back gradually, kept the farm in family hands, and didn't trigger a tax cliff. It also held three weddings, two funerals, and one good apple year.

By Tomás Klauser · Klauser Orchard, gen. III
