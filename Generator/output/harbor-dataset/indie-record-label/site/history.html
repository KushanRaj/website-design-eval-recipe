History,
in chapters.

A label is a strange thing to keep alive for twelve years. This page is the closest we have to an honest account of how we did it — pulled together from notebooks, invoices, voice memos, and the back of a Heidelberg's serial-number plate. Read it slow. There are footnotes.

01
2014

A basement and a 4-track.

The label was started in the basement of a row house on Withers Street, Brooklyn, in the spring of 2014. There were two people, a cassette four-track, a TASCAM mixer that someone had pulled from the street, and a hand-drawn logo that involved a photocopier with a sad face.1 The first catalog number, XRX-001, was a forty-six-minute tape compilation of seven local bands, dubbed in real time on a chain of three borrowed decks.

We pressed forty copies. We sold all forty at a basement show three days later. We used the money to buy a second dual-cassette deck and a box of riso ink. We did not pay ourselves. We have a photograph of the night somewhere — the one where the floor is wet and the kick drum is on top of a milk crate.

The name came from the photocopier. We were borrowing time on a Xerox machine at a print shop two blocks away, running off sleeve inserts at two in the morning. The owner did not know. The owner now sells us paper at cost. *

XRX-001 release night — Withers Street basement, May 2014.

By the end of 2014 the label had pressed five cassettes and one 7-inch lathe-cut. The 7-inch was a Sable Circuit demo, technically — but Sable Circuit didn't exist yet, and the two people who would later form the band were arguing about whether the tape hiss on the lathe-cut was a feature or a defect. We took the position that it was a feature. We still do.

We did not have a website. We had a Tumblr. We had a Bandcamp page that we updated on Tuesdays because Tuesdays were the day we had Wi-Fi at the laundromat. We answered emails by hand from a Hotmail address that we are not going to tell you. We were not, by any definition, a record label. We were two people pressing records. It turned out that was enough.

The sad-face photocopier logo lasted four years. It was replaced in 2018 by the current bracketed wordmark, designed by M. Stamp, who would later art-direct most of the post-2020 sleeves.

02
2017

The first sold-out pressing.

By 2017 the label had a back room with two presses, a borrowed Heidelberg that we still have not given back, and a real address. XRX-014 was the record that broke us out of the basement — a 12-inch by Cyanide Florist that we pressed in a run of three hundred copies on cyan splatter vinyl. We expected it to sell in a year. It sold in five days.2

This was the first time the label had to learn what a second pressing meant. We learned by getting it wrong: the second pressing was a different color, came on a different weight of vinyl, and had a slightly different sleeve, because we ran out of the original ink. Collectors lost their minds. We have since become more careful. We have also become more comfortable making collectors slightly upset.

Vinyl is not a souvenir. It's a unit of work.

We added a sixth catalog category in 2017 — the cassette series, called the Boiler Room Tapes, which paired a roster artist with a guest engineer for a single eight-hour session. The first Boiler Room Tape (XRX-018) was a Mara Vex solo session, recorded the day after her first European tour. The tape was dubbed in real time at the shop. Two hundred copies. Sold out in a weekend.

That same year, we hired our first employee — R. Salinas, who walked into the shop one afternoon with a master tape under one arm and a job application under the other, asked if we needed a mastering engineer, and was hired by 6pm.3 R. is still here. R. mastered every record we have pressed since XRX-019.

Heidelberg in the back room, 2017. We still do not own this machine.

XRX-014 has been repressed four times. We are not pressing it a fifth time.

R. now also runs Pressing Plant 3, the studio side of the label. R. is the reason most of these records sound the way they do.

03
2020

Pressing plants and pandemics.

2020 was the year that the global pressing-plant backlog stretched to fourteen months. It was also the year that we decided, after months of arguing on the back stairs of the shop, to bring more of the work in-house. We bought a used Newbilt press from a label in Cologne that was closing. We installed it in March. We learned how to use it in April, May, June, and most of July. We pressed our first in-house record in August.4

The first in-house pressing was XRX-024, a Sable Circuit 7-inch called Pilot Light. We pressed two hundred copies. About sixty of them had a small surface defect that we eventually identified as a faulty cooling cycle. We sent the defective copies out anyway, with a hand-written letter, calling them "Edition Defect" and charging less. People asked for them specifically. They are now the rarest Sable Circuit record in print.

We learned how to press records on a press, in a basement, during a pandemic, with a 14-month backlog.

The pandemic also forced us to reconsider what a release event was. With no shows happening, we leaned into the dispatch — a long-form email we send once a month, with notes from the shop, photographs from the press room, and a download code for an unreleased track. The dispatch had four hundred subscribers in March 2020. It has thirty-one thousand now. We do not use tracking pixels. We do not run ads in it. We sometimes complain about pressing plants. People seem to like that.

By the end of 2020 the label had pressed eleven records in-house. We were, finally, what the name implied. We were a pressing company. We had taken the photocopier metaphor and shoved a vinyl press under it.

Newbilt press arriving from Cologne, March 2020. Customs called twice.

The press is named Doris, after the woman in Cologne who sold it to us. She had run the same machine for thirty-one years.

04
2023

The Glasgow annex.

In late 2022 we started talking about a second room. Half of our roster was, by then, based in the UK, and the cost of shipping vinyl across the Atlantic had become absurd. We needed a place to press the European editions. We looked at sites in Berlin, in Bristol, in Manchester. We settled on Glasgow because of an empty shop on Trongate Lane, a city council grant we did not expect to get, and a press that another label was giving away in exchange for moving costs.5

The Glasgow annex opened officially in April 2023. It is smaller than the Brooklyn shop — two rooms, one press, one cutting lathe, a kettle, and a couch that A. Liddell pulled from a skip on Saltmarket. We press all of our UK and EU editions there now. The records sound exactly the same. The water in Glasgow is softer, the ink dries differently, and we cannot really explain why the labels print better, but they do.

A second room is not twice as much label. It is a different label, in a different city, sharing a wordmark.

Running two rooms in two countries has not made anything simpler. It has made our shipping cheaper, our turnaround faster, and our weekly all-hands calls extremely funny, because someone is always wearing a coat. We do not plan to open a third room. We have said this before. We will probably do it anyway.

By the time you read this we have pressed forty-seven catalog numbers, signed sixteen artists, employed nine people, paid royalties on time every quarter since 2018, and have not yet missed a release date by more than three weeks. We are not the largest indie label in either city. We are probably not the most important. We are still here. We still press our own records. We still answer email. The dispatch goes out on the first of the month. The door is open Tuesday through Saturday, 11 to 6, Glasgow time.

Glasgow annex, opening day, April 2023. The couch is from a skip.

The other label is still in operation. We send them ink at cost in exchange.

The kettle is a Russell Hobbs. It boils in 38 seconds. We have timed it.

Colophon. Xerox Pressing Co. is run by K. Walsh (Brooklyn) and A. Liddell (Glasgow), with R. Salinas at Pressing Plant 3, M. Stamp on art direction, and a rotating cast of nine other humans who answer email, dub cassettes, and pack boxes.

Mailing address. 47 Withers St., Brooklyn, NY 11211 USA. Glasgow annex at 12c Trongate Lane, Glasgow G1 5HD UK.

Demo policy. Demo inbox is open April 1–30 and October 1–31. Send one link, a paragraph, and a city. We listen to everything. We answer if we can press you.

Pressing. Every record on this label since XRX-024 was pressed by us, on a press named Doris (Brooklyn) or a press named Audrey (Glasgow). We will not be selling Doris.
