Paphiopedilum fairrieanum

Fairrie's Slipper Orchid — a high-elevation slipper orchid from the eastern Himalaya, the standing trophy of the Fernwood orchid bench since 1948.

i.

Overview

Fairrie's Slipper is a high-Himalayan slipper orchid first described from a single plant flowered in Liverpool by John Fairrie in 1857, then thought lost to the wild for half a century. Modern populations cling to wet limestone cliffs in eastern Bhutan and Arunachal Pradesh, where they receive the steady cool mists that this plant — and most orchid growers in the Pacific Northwest — find so agreeable.

At Fernwood it has been our standing trophy since 1948, when our founder's daughter, Genevieve Mór, brought two divisions from a Calcutta merchant. Their descendants now occupy the second bench of Glasshouse III, and each May produce the recurved, sage-and-mahogany flowers that make this species, despite its modest stature, the most photographed plant in the conservatory.

The species name honours its first cultivator. The vernacular ‘Lost Slipper’ honours the half-century during which botanists doubted it still existed in nature.

ii.

Cultivation

Container. A shallow clay pan, never more than two finger-widths wider than the plant. P. fairrieanum resents wet feet and rewards good aeration by flowering at three years from compot.

Medium. We compose ours by volume: six parts medium fir bark, two parts coarse perlite, one part charcoal, one part oyster-shell grit. The mineral component is non-negotiable — the limestone cliffs are the clue.

Water. Rainwater or RO, always at room temperature. We water once the topmost grit-skin is dry to the touch — every three days in summer, every ten in winter. Never leave standing water in the saucer.

Light & air. 12,000 – 18,000 lux from an east-facing bench shaded by a 50% knit cloth in July and August. A small clip-on fan, set to its lowest setting and never aimed at the plant, prevents the fungal blights that punish stagnant growers.

Bench notes — Glasshouse III

  • Spring (Mar – May) — 14 °C nights / 22 °C days · weekly feed at quarter-strength.
  • Summer (Jun – Aug) — vent at 24 °C, fog twice daily, slack off feed.
  • Autumn (Sep – Nov) — slow water, drop nights to 10 °C to initiate flower.
  • Winter (Dec – Feb) — barely moist, no feed, full light through bare glass.
iii.

Propagation

Asymbiotic seed culture is the only ethical and viable route to new plants of P. fairrieanum. Division, while possible on mature specimens of four growths or more, is rarely practised in our nursery — the species sulks for two to four seasons after disturbance.

For seed we follow the Knudson C base, lightly modified with banana homogenate at flask stage, and a sucrose step-down at deflasking. Compot is held for 18 months at 18 °C under fluorescent T5s, then transitioned to the cool bench. From seed to first flower averages 7 – 9 years, which is also the answer to the question patrons most often ask about our prices.

Our annual seedling release (mid-September, members preview the Tuesday prior) is the only legal source of nursery-grown P. fairrieanum in the United States at this time.

iv.

In the Garden

This is, of course, a glasshouse plant for the Pacific Northwest. But the gentle cool-night summer of the Olympic foothills lets us run Glasshouse III a few degrees below most peer institutions, which we believe accounts for the abundance of our flowering.

Patrons may see P. fairrieanum on the second bench of Glasshouse III from late April to early July; specimens are labelled with their accession year and the name of their original grower. The Bench Walk tour (Saturdays, 11.00) brings members past the breeding pans not open to the wider public.

Companion plantings under glass

  • Cypripedium formosanum — for the cool corner of the same bench.
  • Platycerium superbum — overhead, providing the diffused light it likes.
  • Pleione formosana — at its feet, contributing the cooler-spring colour.
  • Bulbophyllum lobbii — same bench, opposite end, for summer contrast.
