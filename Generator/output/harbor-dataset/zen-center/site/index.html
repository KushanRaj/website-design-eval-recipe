A morning at the hall

The fields are not yet full.
The wheat is filling out, slowly.

Pine Tree Hall is a Sōtō Zen monastery in the Eastern Hills, founded in 1985 by Daiun Soshin. The schedule below is what we follow each day, this season, without ceremony. Visitors are welcome at posted hours; please observe silence past the second gate.

I — A sitting

The cushion is set out before the room is lit.

Four-fifty: the wake bell. By five-fifteen, forty cushions are arranged on the long axis of the zendo, each one a little island of darkness on tatami. We sit two periods of thirty-five minutes, separated by a walking period. There is no instruction during sitting. If you have not sat with us before, an introduction is offered Wednesday evenings.

Photograph — long lean of light, west window, late spring.

II — A bell

The han is struck three times before the last sitting of the day.

Of all the sounds in the hall, the han is the one residents learn first to read. Three rolls, descending — the slow gathering toward evening zazen. The board is plain camphor wood; the mallet, kept on a peg by the entry, has shaped its own quiet groove into the surface over forty years.

Photograph — north corridor, beam shadow, half past four.

III — A meal

Oryoki is set out for residents; visitors share a simpler bowl.

Breakfast at six. Rice gruel, pickled radish, a soup of root vegetables — the menu varies slightly with the season but not with the mood of the cook. Residents eat oryoki, the formal Zen practice of taking food. Visitors share an unhurried single bowl in the refectory and follow the chant before and after eating.

Photograph — refectory table, north light, before dawn.
