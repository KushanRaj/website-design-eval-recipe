If you are coming to the hall.

A working monastery is not a museum and not a hotel. Most of what follows is what residents do without thinking; we set it down only so that you can join the rhythm without disturbing it. Read it once before you arrive. We do not test anyone at the gate.

Before you arrive.

Wear loose, dark, plain clothing. Bring socks; the floor is cold even in summer. Leave watches and phones in the bag you carry; they will be set aside at the genkan. We provide cushions; if you have a back injury, bring a small bench you know.

If you arrive after a sitting has begun, wait quietly outside the zendo until kinhin. The doorkeeper, if there is one, will signal you in.

I.

Sanmon Gate三門 — the threshold of silence

The first gate marks the boundary of the monastery grounds; the second, fifty paces in, marks the boundary of speech. Beyond the second gate, please observe silence — including phones, including greetings — until you return.

  • Pass slowly; pause once.
  • Phones already silenced before the first gate.
  • If you must speak, step back to the open courtyard.

II.

Genkan玄関 — entryway, shoes

The genkan is the threshold between the world's clothes and the hall's. Step out of your shoes onto the raised wooden floor, then turn and place your shoes neatly, toes facing the door. Coats and bags go on the pegs and shelves to the right.

  • Shoes off before stepping onto the wooden floor.
  • Phones, watches, wallets: bowl on the left shelf.
  • Robes (if you have your own) are put on in the changing alcove.
  • Hand-wash basin on the right; clean cloths above.

III.

Zendo禅堂 — the meditation hall

Enter on the left side, bow to your seat, turn and bow to the room, then step onto the cushion. During sitting, face the wall. The kyosaku (encouragement stick) is offered only to those who request it by bowing as the jikijitsu passes. Kinhin begins and ends with a bell; follow the person in front of you.

  • Enter on the inhale, with the left foot.
  • Two bows: one to your seat, one to the room.
  • Face the wall during zazen.
  • If you must leave during sitting, do so at kinhin only.
  • No mala, no notebooks, no scented oils.

IV.

Refectory斎堂 — the meal hall

Residents take meals oryoki, a formal practice of nesting bowls, cloths, and chopsticks. Visitors are given a simpler arrangement: one bowl, one spoon, one cloth. The chant before and after eating is on a card at your seat. Eat what is served; what cannot be eaten is set aside silently for the cook.

  • Wait for the bell before unwrapping the bowls.
  • Take only what you will eat.
  • Cleaning water at the end — drink it.
  • Allergies are accommodated; please tell us at arrival.

V.

Garden庭 — samu, the path, the moss

The garden is a working garden, not an exhibit. Residents practice samu — concentrated work — in it twice daily. Visitors are welcome on the stone path, and are asked to step around the moss and the raked gravel. The eastern bench is a sitting place; the western bench is for the night watch.

  • Stay on the stone path.
  • If you wish to join samu, ask the tanto before 07:30.
  • Do not photograph residents at work.
  • Tea is laid out in the small pavilion at 14:30.

By train

Take the Eastern Line to Holloway. From the station, the #14 bus runs hourly to Hollowback Road. Walk uphill ten minutes from the bus stop — keep the stream on your left.

By car

From the highway, take exit 7 and follow signs to Eastern Hills. Park at the lower lot, just below the first gate. Do not drive past the sanmon.

A last note.

Please observe silence past the second gate. If something goes wrong — an injury, a lost child, a question that cannot wait — find a resident in a brown apron; they are the ones authorized to speak with visitors. Everyone else is, today, holding silence as their part of the work.
