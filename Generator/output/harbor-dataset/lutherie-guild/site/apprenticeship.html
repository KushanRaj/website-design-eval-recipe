Cap. III

Of the Path to Mastery

Three tiers, in order: the apprentice at the bench, the journeyman upon the road, the master at the helm of a shop.

It is laid down in the Charter that no maker shall be admitted to the rolls of the Guild who has not first passed through the three formal degrees of instruction, and that the years given to this passage are not to be hurried, foreshortened, nor purchased. The bench will not be cheated. What follows is the present form of the programme, last revised by the Court of Wardens in the twelfth year of His Majesty’s reign.

I. The Apprentice

Duration: Four Years · Stipend: £9,200 per annum + housing

The apprentice is placed within a member workshop by the Court at the spring or autumn intake. The first six months are given over to tools and timber — the truing of planes, the sharpening of chisels at the leather strop, the recognition of figure and grain. From the seventh month the apprentice is permitted, under the master’s eye, to undertake set exercises.

  • Year I — tool fettling, jointing, soundboard splitting
  • Year II — ribs, blocks, linings; first set of f-holes
  • Year III — first complete instrument in white
  • Year IV — varnish, setup, and the Examination of Hands

The Examination is held at the Hall, blind: the apprentice’s instrument is heard behind a curtain by three masters not of the apprentice’s shop.

II. The Journeyman

Duration: Three Years · Stipend: £18,500 per annum + travel grant

The journeyman, having passed the Examination of Hands, is bound to travel. The Guild requires service in at least two further member workshops, in two different countries, and prefers three. The journeyman keeps a Wanderbuch — a bound notebook in which each host master may write a passage of instruction, a recipe, a quibble, or a warning.

  • Minimum twelve months in each host shop
  • One full instrument completed per posting, retained by the host
  • Submission of three pages of bench-notes to the Journal
  • Concluding Address before the Court of Wardens

At the close, the Wanderbuch is bound in the Hall library beside those of every journeyman before, going back to 1721.

III. The Master

Duration: Indefinite · Eligibility: after Journeyman + 7 yr at independent bench

The mastery is not conferred but recognised. After seven years at an independent bench, the journeyman may submit three instruments — in any combination of bowed, plucked or historical — to the Mastership Jury at the Annual Exhibition. The jury sits in closed session for two days and renders its decision in writing.

  • Three instruments submitted blind
  • Recipe-book of the maker’s varnishes deposited with the Hall
  • Verbal defence before five sitting masters
  • Right to receive apprentices upon registration of the house mark

The new master signs the Charter beneath all who have signed before, and is invited to set their own house mark in the Hall’s register.

Footnote †

Stipends as set by the Court of Wardens, MMXXVI. Adjusted annually against the index of bench-tools.

Footnote ‡

Hours at the bench — not less than thirty in any week, including Saturdays half. Sundays are observed for the cleaning and oiling of tools.

Footnote §

The Examination of Hands is conducted in May at the Hall. Apprentices unsuccessful at first sitting may re-present in the following spring.

Footnote ¶

Journeymen are forbidden to undertake commissioned work in their own name until the closing Address. Bench-products from a journeyman year remain the property of the host shop.

Footnote *

A petitioner for mastership must hold the testimony of two members in good standing and present accounts of seven complete years of independent benchwork.

A Petition for Entry to the First Tier

To the Court of Wardens of the Worshipful Guild — the under-signed humbly applieth for admission to the rolls of Apprentice, as set forth in Cap. III of the Charter.

Signum

— Thy petition is received. The Clerk to the Wardens shall reply by post within fourteen days. —
