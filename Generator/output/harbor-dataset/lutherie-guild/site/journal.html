On the Felting of the Bass-Bar — A Re-examination

By Dr. Iola Marrow-Hughes · Atelier Beaufort & Cie., Mirecourt
§ I — The Question

Of what we have inherited

The bass-bar — that long, narrow shaving of spruce glued beneath the foot of the bass-side bridge — has been the subject of more bench-gossip than perhaps any other part of the instrument. Its felting[1][1] By felting we mean the fine longitudinal shaving applied to the underside of the bar before glue-up, the purpose of which is to compensate for irregular contact at the plate. See Sacconi, I ‘Segreti’ di Stradivari, 1972, pp. 124–128. — the long, hand-planed taper applied just before fitting — has been passed from master to apprentice in five or six distinct variants, of which the present author has examined four at length.

The question lately raised at the Cremona meeting is whether the well-known ‘Sacconi’ treatment, taught now in almost every member workshop, truly serves the lower voices of modern strings as well as we have supposed.[2][2] Cremona Convocation, MMXXV. The dissenting paper was given by Maestro G. M. Galliani, who held that the present standard over-stiffens the G-string for new gut. The present paper offers a small contribution, based on twenty bench-fittings made in the past two seasons, eight under the standard recipe and twelve under a modified one which the author has dubbed the Mirecourt soft-foot.

§ II — The Method

Of the bench-trials

Twenty violins were prepared, all of broadly Strad pattern, in the workshop of Beaufort & Cie., between the spring of MMXXIV and the autumn of MMXXV. Eight received the standard Sacconi treatment, twelve the modified method. Each was strung in identical gut from Aquila, tested in a single hall under stable humidity, and recorded for ten seconds at each open string by a sitting panel of three masters and one violinist of long standing.[3][3] Panellists: M. Galliani (Cremona), C. Thorne (Edinburgh), O. Adebayo (Montréal); player Y. Tanaka-Olsen, soloist, Royal Northern Sinfonia.

The recordings were then admitted to spectral analysis by a method described by Cremer (1981) and lately refined by the present author in Journal, Vol. CCCIX. The results are gathered in Fig. II overleaf, and discussed at length in Section IV of the present paper.

§ III — The Result

Of the modified foot

The soft-foot’s effect, on this small sample, is a marginal enrichment of the second formant of the lowest string, with no measurable cost above the C of the staff. The panel, blind to the treatment, preferred the modified instruments at a ratio of seven to four when listening on the G-string alone; the preference vanished above D. We make no claim that the result is general — twenty instruments is a small sample — but offer the figures for the consideration of the membership.[4][4] The full bench-notes, including dimensions, masses, and varnish recipes, are deposited at the Hall and available for inspection by appointment with the Clerk.

§ IV — Discussion & Caveat

Of what cannot yet be said

It is the present author’s reading of these results that the ‘Sacconi’ bar, well-loved as it is, may have been calibrated for an earlier gauge of string and a slightly stiffer bridge than is now standard. The Mirecourt soft-foot appears to recover some quiet ground at the lower voices. Whether the effect persists across instruments of other patterns, in other halls, under other players — that is the matter of the next twenty fittings. The author solicits replications from the membership and welcomes correspondence at the Hall.

Iola Marrow-Hughes · D.Phil. (Cantab.), Master Luthier Lately of the Atelier Beaufort, Mirecourt. Author of The Vibrating Plate: A Workbench Treatise (Hall Press, MMXXIV) and contributor to this Journal since MMXVIII.
