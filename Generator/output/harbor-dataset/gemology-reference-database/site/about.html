Our Mission

About the Reference

A curated editorial archive built by gemologists, mineralogists, and scholars — for collectors, designers, students, and anyone captivated by the rarity and beauty of the mineral world.

Editorial Statement

A Collector's Archive for the Digital Age

The Gemology Reference Database was conceived as a response to the fragmented state of gemological knowledge in the digital realm — where authoritative data sits scattered across academic journals, trade publications, laboratory reports, and the closely guarded expertise of dealers and specialists who have spent careers building knowledge that rarely reaches the collector or student who needs it most.

"Every exceptional gem is a message from deep geological time — a crystalline record of pressure, temperature, chemistry, and chance that took millions of years to compose and mere decades for a skilled cutter to reveal."

Our editorial approach treats gemology as the discipline it is: a rigorous intersection of mineralogy, optical physics, materials science, economic geology, and cultural history. We do not traffic in superlatives or marketing language. A "commercial quality" stone is described as such; the premium commanded by an unheated ruby is stated with its commercial rationale.

What This Reference Covers

The database spans eight principal reference sections: the Mineral Specimen Index catalogues physical constants and origin data for 342 gem species and varieties; the Grading section provides comparative scales for the major gem types; the Cutting Styles Gallery documents 87 faceting styles with proportion data and historical context; the Geological Origins Map plots 214 documented deposit locations with geological period and deposit-type data; the Collector's Glossary defines over 1,240 terms from adularescence to zoning; and the Certification Guides provide practical buyer guidance for navigating laboratory reports and treatment disclosure.

Scope and Limitations

This reference covers faceted gemstones and mineral specimens of collector interest. Organic gems (pearl, coral, amber, jet) are referenced in the glossary but do not have full specimen pages in the current edition. Meteoritic gems (moldavite, make-earth olivine) are catalogued but without full grading scales. Synthetic and simulant identification is referenced in certification guides but this database is primarily oriented toward natural material.

Grading data reflects industry consensus as of the current publication date (May 2026) and may not reflect unpublished laboratory methodology revisions. Origin determination criteria evolve as new deposits are discovered and analytical techniques improve — users are advised to consult current laboratory documentation for the most recent origin determination protocols.

Editorial Standards

Data Sourcing & Methodology

◆
Primary Laboratory Sources

Physical constants (RI, SG, hardness, crystal system) are drawn from current GIA publications, the Gemological Institute of America's Colored Stone Grading system, and peer-reviewed mineralogical reference works including Deer, Howie & Zussman Rock-Forming Minerals and Gübelin & Koivula Photoatlas of Inclusions in Gemstones.

●
Grading Scale Standards

Color and clarity grades follow GIA standards for diamond and AGTA guidelines for colored stones. Where grading terminology varies between laboratories (GIA, Gübelin, SSEF, AGL), equivalency mappings are noted with explicit caveats about laboratory-specific methodology differences. Grade equivalencies are approximate and should not be used as a substitute for independent laboratory assessment.

▲
Origin Deposit Data

Geographic deposit locations are compiled from published geological surveys, USGS mineral resources data, peer-reviewed papers in Gems & Gemology, The Journal of Gemmology, and Mineralogical Magazine. Coordinate data is approximate to 25km for general educational reference and does not represent survey-grade geological mapping.

■
Trade Value Premiums

Value premium statements (e.g., "Kashmir sapphires command X% premium") are derived from published auction results (Christie's, Sotheby's, Bonhams) and trade survey data from Gemfields, Rapaport, and GemSelect market reports. Premium data reflects approximate ranges as of the publication date and is not investment advice.

◉
Peer Review Process

All new entries and substantial revisions are reviewed by at least one credentialed gemologist (FGA, GIA GG, or PhD mineralogy) before publication. Treatment disclosure data is reviewed against current CIBJO guidelines and laboratory disclosure standards. The editorial team endeavors to update entries within 90 days of significant industry changes (new laboratory methodology publications, CIBJO rule updates, major new deposit discoveries).

Get in Touch

Contact & Submission Guidelines

Editorial Corrections

Found a factual error, outdated reference, or data discrepancy? We welcome corrections from credentialed gemologists and mineralogists. Please include the specific entry, the correction, and a published source reference. Corrections are reviewed within 14 business days.

New Entry Submissions

Contributing a new gem species, cut style, deposit profile, or glossary term? Submissions should follow the reference format documented in our contributor guidelines and be accompanied by at least two corroborating published sources (GIA, peer-reviewed journal, or major laboratory publication).

Licensing & Citation

All reference data is provided for educational and personal use. Academic citation: Gemology Reference Database (2026). Commercial licensing for dealer or institutional use is available — inquire through the editorial office with details of intended use and distribution scope.

Image Contributions

High-resolution mineral specimen photographs and facet diagram illustrations are always needed. We accept contributions under Creative Commons Attribution license. Images must include species, variety, carat weight where known, and photographed collection attribution.
