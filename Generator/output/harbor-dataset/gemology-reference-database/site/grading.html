Grading & Certification

Comparative grading scales, clarity charts, cut proportion reference, and multi-laboratory certification equivalency data for major gem species.

Emeralds are graded using a combination of color quality (hue, tone, saturation), clarity (jardin assessment), and cut. Colombian material is the benchmark against which all other origins are evaluated.

Color Grading

Emerald Color Scale

Pale / Insignificant Light Medium Light Medium Medium Dark Dark Very Dark

Premium Color Standard

A fine Colombian emerald displays vivid, slightly bluish-green to pure green hue with medium to medium-dark tone (60–75%) and strong saturation — the "Muzo Green" benchmark.

Ruby grading centers on color quality — particularly the chromium-driven red to slightly purplish-red hue. The Burmese "Pigeon's Blood" standard commands 50–100% premiums over comparable material from other origins.

Blue sapphire color grading follows the "Royal Blue" to "Cornflower Blue" spectrum. Kashmir material sets the benchmark for velvety appearance; Burmese sapphires typically show more intensity while Ceylon stones display brighter, lighter tones.

Color Grading

Blue Sapphire Color Scale

Pale Blue Medium Light Cornflower Royal Blue Kashmir Blue Very Dark Inky

Kashmir Standard

Fine Kashmir sapphires display a characteristic velvety, slightly sleepy quality with intensely saturated medium to medium-dark blue (60–75% tone) — caused by dense rutile needle inclusions scattering light.

Diamond grading follows the GIA 4Cs system: Color (D–Z scale), Clarity (FL–I3), Cut (Excellent–Poor), and Carat weight. The GIA system is the global standard; AGS, HRD, and IGI maintain comparable but not identical scales.

Alexandrite grading uniquely includes color-change assessment: the quality of the color shift between incandescent and daylight illumination is the primary value driver. Fine specimens show a complete shift from vivid green (daylight) to intense red (incandescent).

Tourmaline grading varies significantly by variety. Paraíba tourmalines (copper-bearing elbaites) are graded against a "neon" vivid blue-green standard and command extraordinary premiums based on copper/manganese content and color saturation.
